0 The Blue-winged Warbler, Vermivora cyanoptera, is a fairly common New World warbler, 11.5 cm long and weighing 8.5 g. It breeds in eastern North America in southern Ontario and the eastern USA.
0 Its range is extending northwards, where it is replacing the very closely related Golden-winged Warbler, Vermivora chrysoptera.
0 It was formerly called Vermivora pinus, but this name is invalid, as it was given after descriptions of this species and the Pine Warbler.
0 It is migratory, wintering in southern Central America.
0 It is a very rare vagrant to western Europe, with one bird wandering to Ireland.
1 The breeding male Blue-winged Warbler is unmistakable.
1 It is yellow above and below.
1 The wings are gray with two white bars, and there is a black eye stripe.
1 Females are duller, but otherwise similar.
0 The breeding habitat is open scrubby areas.
0 Blue-winged Warblers nest on the ground or low in a bush, laying 4-7 eggs in a cup nest.
0 These birds feed on insects, and spiders.
0 The song is a series of buzzing notes.
0 The call is a sharp chip.
0 This species forms two distinctive hybrids with Golden-winged Warbler.
0 The commoner, genetically dominant Brewster's Warbler is gray above and whitish or yellow below.
0 It has a black eye stripe and two white wing bars.
0 The rarer recessive Lawrence's Warbler has a male plumage which is green and yellow above and yellow below, with white wing bars and the same face pattern as male Golden-winged.
0 The female is gray above and whitish below with two yellow wing bars and the same face pattern as female Golden-winged.
