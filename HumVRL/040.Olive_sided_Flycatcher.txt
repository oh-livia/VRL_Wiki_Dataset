0 The Olive-sided Flycatcher, Contopus cooperi, is a passerine bird.
1 It is a medium-sized tyrant flycatcher.
1 Adults are dark olive on the face, upperparts and flanks.
1 They have light underparts, a large dark bill and a short tail.
0 The song is a whistled quick-three beers.
0 The call is a rapid pip pip pip.
0 Contopus borealis is a junior synonym of Contopus cooperi, according to the 1997 AOU checklist, quoted by BISON.
0 The name of this species is listed as Contopus borealis in many older guides.
0 Their breeding habitat is coniferous woods across Canada, Alaska and the northeastern and western United States, and other types of wooded area in California.
0 Olive-sided Flycatchers are abundant in early postfire landscapes that have burned at high severity.
0 These birds migrate to Central America and the Andes region of South America.
0 They wait on a perch at the top of a tree and fly out to catch insects in flight.
0 The female usually lays 3 eggs in a shallow open cup nest on a horizontal tree branch.
0 The male defends a large area around the nesting territory.
0 Both parents feed the young birds.
0 The numbers of this bird are declining, probably due to loss of habitat in its winter range.
